Subsurface eddies have been widely documented throughout the global ocean. Unlike surface-intensified cold-core cyclonic eddies and warm-core anticylonic eddies, they often display opposite or even absent temperature signatures at the sea surface. Recent studies have suggested the existence of a transformation process between these two eddy types. In this study, we propose a new mechanism to explain this process, highlighting the pivotal role of background non-parallel isopycnals in facilitating eddy transitions. First, using output from an eddy-resolving ocean general circulation model, we tracked and analyzed a cyclonic eddy (CE) transition event. Theresults demonstrate that isopycnal-divergent variations in the background primarily account for the reversal of sea surface temperature anomalies (SSTA) and the enhancement of subsurface eddy kinetic energy (EKE) and vorticity. This baroclinic background is characterized by non-parallel and opposite isopycnal slopes between the surface and subsurface layers. Second, to further justify this mechanism, we performed a suite of idealized numerical experiments which are initialized with surface-intensified eddies and without external forcing. The results are consistent with theoretical expectations and emphasize the essential role of the non-parallel background isopycnals in driving the surface-to-subsurface transformation.
